Vast majority of malware attacks spawned from legit sites
from Ars Technica by http://arstechnica.com <div id="rss-wrap"> <div> <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/google-malicious-website-data-640x345.jpg"><div class="caption" style="font-size:0.8em"> <div class="caption-credit"> Google </div> </div> </div> <p>The vast majority of sites that push malware on their visitors are legitimate online services that have been hacked as opposed to those hosted by attackers for the purposes of distributing malicious software, Google security researchers said Tuesday.</p> <p>The data, included for the first time as part of the <a href="http://www.google.com/transparencyreport/safebrowsing">safe browsing section</a> of Google's regular transparency report, further challenges the myth that malware attacks happen only on disreputable sites, such as those that peddle porn, illicit software ("warez"), and similar content. For instance, on June 9 only 3,891 of the sites Google blocked as part of its <a href="http://googleonlinesecurity.blogspot.com/2012/06/safe-browsing-protecting-web-users-for.html">Safe Browsing program</a> were dedicated malware sites, while the remaining 39,247 sites that were filtered offered legitimate services that had been compromised.</p> <p>In all, Google blocks about 10,000 sites per day as part of the program, which is designed to help people using Firefox, Chrome, and other participating browsers to steer clear of phishing scams and drive-by malware attacks. The program is also designed to inform webmasters of infections hitting their site and to take steps to fix the problems. In all, the Safe Browsing program helps protect about 1 billion people per day.</p> </div><p><a href="http://arstechnica.com/security/2013/06/vast-majority-of-malware-attacks-spawned-from-legit-sites/#p3">Read 2 remaining paragraphs</a> | <a href="http://arstechnica.com/security/2013/06/vast-majority-of-malware-attacks-spawned-from-legit-sites/?comments=1">Comments</a></p><img src="http://feeds.feedburner.com/~r/arstechnica/everything/~4/_ndPPR-K7Z4" height="1" width="1"/>

It wasn’t a victim of sea level rise or a David Copperfield illusion—there just wasn’t anything there. A lot of the news coverage at the time was (quite appropriately) of the “Gee—whaddya know?” variety, but the researchers who “undiscovered” (<i>de-scovered?</i>) Sandy Island recently published a paper in <i>Eos </i>detailing answers to deeper questions—how did the island get on the map in the first place and what can we learn from its undiscovery?</p> <p>The vessel, the RV <i>Southern Surveyor</i>, wasn’t out checking on islands to make sure they were still there. But it turns out that crop rotation can also help control some agricultural pests. Many pests are specialized feeders that grow on a limited number of plants. Place a distantly related species in the same field, and the pests find themselves with nothing to eat. By the time the original crop rotates back into the field, the bugs that specialize in feeding on it are long since dead.</p> <p>This approach helped effectively control the western corn rootworm. As its name implies, the animal specializes in eating corn (as the name <em>doesn't</em> imply, it's actually a beetle). By alternating corn and soybeans in the same field, the corn rootworm ended up spending every other year with nothing to eat, keeping its numbers in check. Unfortunately, Illinois has seen the appearance of a "rotation resistant" strain, which is now spreading more widely across the US.</p> <p>How does an insect become resistant to the fact that its normal meal vanishes? Hulu and YouTube were dismissed from the case, while WildTangent had to fight on. WildTangent challenged Ultramercial's "invention" as being too abstract to qualify for patent protection, and the company won a ruling from US District Court in Central California dismissing the patent lawsuit.</p> <p>The appeals court reversed the district court decision and remanded the case to that court. Cassini captures gigantic hurricane on Saturn in exquisite detail
Cassini captures gigantic hurricane on Saturn in exquisite detail
from Ars Technica by http://arstechnica.com <div id="rss-wrap"> <div> <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/PIA12826_modest.jpg"><div class="caption" style="font-size:0.8em"> <div class="caption-credit"> <a rel="nofollow" href="http://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/catalog/PIA12826">NASA/JPL</a> </div> </div> </div> <p>Jupiter's Great Red Spot may get most of the attention, but it's hardly the only big weather event in the Solar System. Saturn, for example, has an odd hexagonal pattern in the clouds at its north pole, and when the planet tilted enough to illuminate it, the light revealed <a href="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/news/news.php?release=2013-149">a giant hurricane</a> embedded in the center of the hexagon. Scientists think the immense storm may have been there for years.</p> <p>But Saturn is also home to transient storms that show up sporadically. The most notable of these are the Great White Spots, which can persist for months and alter the weather on a planetary scale. Great White Spots are rare, with only six having been observed since 1876. When one formed in 2010, we were lucky enough to have the Cassini orbiter in place to watch it from close up. Even though the head of the storm was roughly 7,000 km across, Cassini's cameras were able to image it at resolutions where each pixel was only 14 km across, allowing an unprecedented view into the storm's dynamics.</p> <p>The storm turned out to be very violent, with convective features as big as 3,000 km across that could form and dissipate in as little as 10 hours. Winds of over 400 km/hour were detected, and the pressure gradient between the storm and the unaffected areas nearby was twice that of the one observed in the Great Red Spot of Jupiter. By carefully mapping the direction of the winds, the authors were able to conclude that the head of the White Spot was an anti-cyclone, with winds orbiting around a central feature.</p> </div><p><a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2013/06/cassini-captures-gigantic-hurricane-on-saturn-in-exquisite-detail/#p3">Read 4 remaining paragraphs</a> | <a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2013/06/cassini-captures-gigantic-hurricane-on-saturn-in-exquisite-detail/?comments=1">Comments</a></p><img src="http://feeds.feedburner.com/~r/arstechnica/everything/~4/5qqmXf-ipg4" height="1" width="1"/>

Samsung launches three different flavors of its Galaxy Tab 3
from Ars Technica by http://arstechnica.com <div id="rss-wrap"> <div class="image center large"> <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/gtab3whitegalaxy-tab-3-7-sidemat.jpg"><img src="/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/gtab3whitegalaxy-tab-3-7-sidemat-640x426.jpg"></a><div class="caption" style="font-size:0.8em"> <div class="caption-text"><a href="/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/gtab3whitegalaxy-tab-3-7-sidemat.jpg" class="caption-link" rel="nofollow"></a></div> </div> </div> <p>Samsung has introduced three different sizes of the Galaxy Tab 3 in the hopes that there's at least one that fits you just right. The company <a href="http://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20130624005824/en/Samsung-Galaxy-Tab-3-Portfolio-Offers-Consumers">announced</a> today that its US customers will be able to buy a 7-inch tablet for $199, an 8-inch option for $299, or 10.1-inch option for $399.</p> <p>Samsung writes that the "portfolio" of tablets is supposed to offer consumers "more options," but one could also view the move as the company hedging its bets. With a 7-inch model, for instance, Samsung can compete head-to-head with the ever-popular Asus Nexus 7 while also offering varying sizes for those who might want a larger form factor—or a choice over other similarly sized Android tablets.</p> <p>Your choice is not limited to screen size, however. The Galaxy Tab 3 7.0 will <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2013/06/24/samsung-galaxy-tab-3-7-0-8-0-and-10-1-release-date-pricing/?utm_medium=feed&amp;utm_source=Feed_Classic&amp;utm_campaign=Engadget">feature</a> a dual-core 1.2GHz processor, 8GB of storage with expandable memory, a 1024×600 resolution TFT display, and Android 4.1 Jelly Bean. The 8-inch variant features slightly better specifications, with an Exynos 1.5GHz dual-core processor with 1.5GB of RAM, a 1280×800 resolution TFT display, and Android 4.2 Jelly Bean, as well as a rear-facing 5-megapixel camera and 16GB of storage. The last of the family of new tablets borrows from each of its siblings, sporting the same resolution as the 8-inch Galaxy Tab 3 and the same camera specifications as the 7-inch model. The biggest difference is its Intel Z2560 1.6GHz dual-core processor, though it only has 1GB of RAM.</p> </div><p><a href="http://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2013/06/samsung-launches-three-different-flavors-of-its-galaxy-tab-3/#p3">Read 2 remaining paragraphs</a> | <a href="http://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2013/06/samsung-launches-three-different-flavors-of-its-galaxy-tab-3/?comments=1">Comments</a></p><img src="http://feeds.feedburner.com/~r/arstechnica/everything/~4/szl4cs5mjJ8" height="1" width="1"/>
